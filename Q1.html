US Healthcare & Medical Billing

1. What is the US Healthcare System?

The US healthcare system is a combination of private insurance, employer-sponsored plans, and government programs such as Medicare and Medicaid. Providers deliver healthcare services, while insurance companies process claims and reimburse covered medical expenses according to the patient's health plan benefits.

2. Who are the major participants in the US Healthcare System?

The major participants include patients, healthcare providers, insurance companies (payers), employers, government programs, pharmacies, laboratories, and medical billing professionals. Together they ensure patients receive medical care, claims are processed correctly, and providers receive payment for covered healthcare services.

3. What is medical billing?

Medical billing is the process of preparing, submitting, tracking, and following up insurance claims after healthcare services are provided. It ensures providers receive timely reimbursement while accurately determining the patient's financial responsibility according to the insurance policy.

4. What is medical coding?

Medical coding converts diagnoses, procedures, medical services, and supplies into standardized codes such as ICD-10-CM, CPT, and HCPCS. Accurate coding supports proper claim submission, reimbursement, healthcare reporting, and compliance with insurance and regulatory requirements.

5. What is ICD-10-CM?

ICD-10-CM stands for International Classification of Diseases, Tenth Revision, Clinical Modification. It is used to report diagnoses, diseases, injuries, symptoms, and medical conditions. These codes explain why the patient received healthcare services.

6. What is a CPT code?

CPT stands for Current Procedural Terminology. CPT codes identify medical procedures and professional services performed by physicians and other qualified healthcare providers. They describe what service was performed during the patient's visit.

7. What is HCPCS?

HCPCS stands for Healthcare Common Procedure Coding System. Level I includes CPT codes, while Level II identifies medical equipment, supplies, ambulance services, prosthetics, orthotics, and certain medications that are not described by CPT codes.

8. What is the difference between ICD, CPT, and HCPCS?

ICD codes explain why the patient received treatment by identifying diagnoses. CPT codes describe what medical procedure or service was performed. HCPCS Level II codes identify supplies, equipment, ambulance transportation, prosthetics, and certain medications used during patient care.

9. What is an insurance claim?

An insurance claim is a request submitted by a healthcare provider to an insurance company seeking payment for medical services provided to an insured patient. Claims contain diagnosis codes, procedure codes, patient information, and provider details.

10. What is an EOB?

EOB stands for Explanation of Benefits. It is issued by the insurance company after processing a claim. It explains billed charges, allowed amount, insurance payment, adjustments, and the patient's financial responsibility. An EOB is not a medical bill.

11. What is ERA?

ERA stands for Electronic Remittance Advice. It is the electronic version of claim payment information sent by an insurance company to a healthcare provider. It contains payment details, adjustments, denial reasons, and remark codes, allowing medical billers to post insurance payments accurately into billing software.

12. What is Patient Responsibility?

Patient responsibility is the portion of a healthcare bill that the patient must pay after insurance processes the claim. It may include deductible, copayment, coinsurance, non-covered services, or charges exceeding the insurance plan's benefits. Medical billers collect this remaining balance from the patient.

13. What is a Deductible?

A deductible is the amount a patient must pay for covered healthcare services before the insurance company begins paying according to the policy. Once the deductible has been met, the insurance company usually shares costs through copayments or coinsurance, depending on the health plan.

14. What is Copayment (Copay)?

A copayment, commonly called a copay, is a fixed amount a patient pays for a covered healthcare service. For example, a patient may pay $25 for a primary care visit or $50 for a specialist visit, while the insurance company pays the remaining covered amount.

15. What is Coinsurance?

Coinsurance is the percentage of the allowed medical expense that a patient pays after meeting the deductible. For example, in an 80/20 plan, the insurance company pays 80 percent of the allowed amount, while the patient pays the remaining 20 percent.

16. What is Prior Authorization?

Prior authorization is approval obtained from an insurance company before certain medical procedures, surgeries, medications, or diagnostic tests are performed. If required authorization is not received in advance, the insurance company may deny or reduce payment for the service.

17. What is a Referral?

A referral is a recommendation from a Primary Care Physician (PCP) directing a patient to receive treatment from a specialist. Many managed care plans require referrals before specialist visits to ensure medically appropriate care and proper insurance coverage.

18. What is Revenue Cycle Management (RCM)?

Revenue Cycle Management is the complete financial process followed by healthcare organizations. It begins with patient registration and insurance verification, continues through coding, billing, claim submission, payment posting, denial management, and concludes with collecting any remaining patient balance.

19. What is a Claim Denial?

A claim denial occurs when an insurance company refuses payment for all or part of a submitted claim. Common reasons include incorrect coding, missing information, lack of medical necessity, expired coverage, duplicate claims, or failure to obtain prior authorization.

20. What is a Claim Appeal?

A claim appeal is a formal request asking an insurance company to review and reconsider a denied or underpaid claim. The provider submits supporting medical documentation, corrected coding, or additional evidence to justify payment for the healthcare services provided.

21. What is HIPAA?

HIPAA (Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act) is a United States federal law enacted in 1996 to protect the privacy and security of patient health information. It establishes standards for handling Protected Health Information (PHI) and requires healthcare organizations to implement safeguards against unauthorized access or disclosure.

22. What is Protected Health Information (PHI)?

Protected Health Information (PHI) includes any identifiable information related to a patient's health condition, treatment, or payment for healthcare. Examples include patient names, medical record numbers, diagnoses, laboratory reports, insurance information, and billing records maintained by healthcare organizations.

23. What is the HIPAA Privacy Rule?

The HIPAA Privacy Rule protects patient information by regulating how PHI is used and disclosed. It gives patients important rights over their medical information and requires healthcare providers, health plans, and business associates to maintain confidentiality while performing healthcare operations.

24. What is the HIPAA Security Rule?

The HIPAA Security Rule specifically protects Electronic Protected Health Information (ePHI). It requires administrative, physical, and technical safeguards such as employee training, secure facilities, passwords, encryption, and controlled system access to protect electronic health records from unauthorized use.

25. What is the Minimum Necessary Standard?

The Minimum Necessary Standard requires workforce members to access, use, or disclose only the minimum amount of PHI needed to perform a specific job duty. This helps reduce unnecessary exposure of patient information and strengthens privacy protection within healthcare organizations.

26. What are Covered Entities under HIPAA?

Covered Entities are organizations that must comply with HIPAA requirements. They include healthcare providers, hospitals, physicians, health insurance companies, and healthcare clearinghouses that create, receive, maintain, or transmit Protected Health Information during healthcare operations.

27. What is a Business Associate?

A Business Associate is an individual or company that performs services for a Covered Entity and may access Protected Health Information. Examples include medical billing companies, coding companies, cloud storage providers, IT support vendors, and claims processing organizations.

28. What are common reasons for claim denial?

Claims may be denied because of incorrect diagnosis or procedure codes, missing documentation, invalid patient information, inactive insurance coverage, duplicate claims, lack of prior authorization, non-covered services, or filing the claim after the payer's submission deadline.

29. How can claim denials be prevented?

Claim denials can be reduced by verifying insurance eligibility, obtaining prior authorization when required, using accurate ICD, CPT, and HCPCS codes, submitting complete documentation, correcting claim errors before submission, and following payer-specific billing guidelines and filing deadlines.

30. What qualities make a successful Medical Billing Professional?

A successful medical billing professional has strong knowledge of medical terminology, coding systems, insurance guidelines, HIPAA compliance, attention to detail, communication skills, analytical thinking, problem-solving ability, computer proficiency, and a commitment to accuracy, confidentiality, and continuous learning.

31. What is Medicare?

Medicare is a federal health insurance program primarily for people aged 65 and older, certain younger individuals with disabilities, and patients with End-Stage Renal Disease. It includes Part A, Part B, Part C, and Part D coverage options.

32. What is Medicaid?

Medicaid is a joint federal and state healthcare program that provides medical coverage for eligible low-income individuals and families. Benefits and eligibility requirements vary by state, but it helps millions access healthcare services and prescription medications.

33. What is Medical Necessity?

Medical necessity refers to healthcare services or procedures that are appropriate, reasonable, and required to diagnose or treat a patient's illness or condition according to accepted medical standards. Insurance companies often review medical necessity before approving claims.

34. What is Eligibility Verification?

Eligibility verification is the process of confirming that a patient's insurance coverage is active before services are provided. It helps determine benefits, copays, deductibles, coinsurance, policy limitations, and authorization requirements.

35. What is Coordination of Benefits (COB)?

Coordination of Benefits determines the order in which multiple insurance plans pay claims. One plan acts as the primary payer while another acts as the secondary payer, helping prevent duplicate payments and ensuring proper reimbursement.

36. What is an Allowed Amount?

The allowed amount is the maximum payment an insurance company recognizes for a covered healthcare service. It may be lower than the provider's billed charge. Insurance payments, coinsurance, and patient responsibility are calculated using this amount.

37. What is a Contractual Adjustment?

A contractual adjustment is the difference between the provider's billed charge and the insurance company's negotiated allowed amount. Providers usually write off this amount when services are performed by in-network providers according to payer contracts.

38. What is an NPI?

NPI stands for National Provider Identifier. It is a unique 10-digit identification number assigned to healthcare providers in the United States. It is used on claims, electronic transactions, and healthcare records.

39. What is a Healthcare Clearinghouse?

A healthcare clearinghouse acts as an intermediary between providers and insurance companies. It reviews claims for errors, converts claim formats when necessary, and transmits claims electronically to the appropriate payer for processing.

40. Why is Documentation Important in Medical Billing?

Documentation supports diagnosis codes, procedure codes, medical necessity, reimbursement, compliance, and legal requirements. Accurate documentation reduces claim denials, supports audits, improves patient care, and ensures providers receive correct payment.

41. What is Revenue Cycle Management (RCM)?

Revenue Cycle Management (RCM) is the complete financial process used by healthcare organizations. It begins with patient registration and insurance verification, continues through medical coding, billing, claim submission, payment posting, denial management, and ends with collecting any remaining patient balance.

42. What is Payment Posting?

Payment posting is the process of recording payments received from insurance companies and patients into the medical billing system. Billers compare payments with the ERA or EOB, apply contractual adjustments, and identify any remaining patient responsibility or claim discrepancies.

43. What is Accounts Receivable (AR) in Medical Billing?

Accounts Receivable (AR) refers to unpaid claims or outstanding balances owed to a healthcare provider by insurance companies or patients. AR specialists monitor aging reports, follow up on unpaid claims, resolve denials, and ensure timely reimbursement.

44. What is a Timely Filing Limit?

A timely filing limit is the maximum period allowed by an insurance company to submit a medical claim after services are provided. Claims submitted after this deadline may be denied unless an exception applies according to payer policies.

45. What is Medical Coding Compliance?

Medical coding compliance means assigning diagnosis and procedure codes accurately according to official coding guidelines, payer requirements, and healthcare regulations. Proper compliance helps prevent fraud, reduces claim denials, supports correct reimbursement, and maintains high documentation standards.

46. What is Fraud in Medical Billing?

Medical billing fraud is the intentional submission of false or misleading information to obtain improper payment from an insurance company or government healthcare program. Examples include billing for services not provided, upcoding, duplicate billing, and falsifying medical records.

47. What is Upcoding?

Upcoding occurs when a healthcare provider or biller reports a procedure or service with a higher-paying code than the service actually performed. This practice is considered fraudulent and may result in claim audits, financial penalties, repayment obligations, and legal action.

48. What is Downcoding?

Downcoding is the assignment of a lower-level procedure code than the service actually performed. It can reduce reimbursement for providers and may occur because of coding errors, insufficient documentation, or payer claim processing decisions.

49. Why is Accuracy Important in Medical Billing?

Accuracy is essential because it ensures correct reimbursement, minimizes claim denials, maintains compliance with healthcare regulations, protects patient information, supports proper documentation, and improves the financial health of healthcare organizations. Accurate billing also increases patient trust and reduces administrative delays.

50. How do you prepare for a Medical Billing Interview?

Prepare by reviewing medical terminology, ICD-10-CM, CPT, HCPCS, HIPAA, insurance concepts, EOB, ERA, deductibles, copayments, coinsurance, claim processing, denial management, and revenue cycle management. Practice answering interview questions clearly, confidently, and with real-world examples whenever possible.
