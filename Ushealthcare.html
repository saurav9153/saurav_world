Medical Billing & Healthcare Fundamentals

Introduction

The United States Healthcare System is one of the world's most advanced healthcare systems. Unlike many countries that have a single government-funded healthcare system, the United States uses a combination of private insurance, government insurance programs, employers, healthcare providers, and patients.

Medical billing professionals play an important role by ensuring healthcare providers receive payment for services rendered while maintaining accurate patient records and insurance claims.

Main Participants of US Healthcare System

Participant Role
Patient Receives medical treatment and healthcare services.
Provider Doctors, hospitals, clinics, laboratories and healthcare facilities.
Insurance Company (Payer) Pays eligible medical expenses according to insurance policy.
Employer Often provides health insurance benefits to employees.
Government Provides Medicare, Medicaid and regulates healthcare laws.

Types of Health Insurance

  • Private Insurance – Purchased individually or through employers.
  • Employer Sponsored Insurance (ESI) – Health insurance provided by employers.
  • Medicare – Federal program mainly for people aged 65 years and older or certain disabled individuals.
  • Medicaid – State and federal program for eligible low-income individuals and families.
  • TRICARE – Healthcare for military members and their families.
  • CHIP – Children's Health Insurance Program.
  • VA Healthcare – Healthcare services for eligible veterans.

Healthcare Delivery Process

  1. Patient schedules appointment.
  2. Patient registration and insurance verification.
  3. Doctor examines patient.
  4. Diagnosis is made.
  5. Treatment is provided.
  6. Medical documentation is completed.
  7. Medical coder assigns diagnosis and procedure codes.
  8. Medical biller submits insurance claim.
  9. Insurance processes the claim.
  10. Provider receives payment.
  11. Patient pays remaining balance if applicable.

What is HIPAA?

HIPAA stands for Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act. It is a United States federal law enacted in 1996 to protect patients' medical information and establish national standards for healthcare privacy and security.

HIPAA ensures that healthcare providers, insurance companies, clearinghouses, and their business partners properly protect sensitive patient information from unauthorized access, disclosure, or misuse.

History of HIPAA

Year Event
1996 HIPAA signed into law.
2003 Privacy Rule became effective.
2005 Security Rule implemented.
2009 HITECH Act strengthened HIPAA protections.
2013 Omnibus Rule expanded patient privacy rights.

Main Objectives of HIPAA

  • Protect patient privacy.
  • Maintain confidentiality of health records.
  • Improve healthcare efficiency.
  • Reduce healthcare fraud and abuse.
  • Secure electronic medical records.
  • Standardize healthcare transactions.
  • Give patients greater control over their health information.

HIPAA Privacy Rule

The HIPAA Privacy Rule establishes standards for protecting patients' medical information and limits how Protected Health Information (PHI) may be used or disclosed.

Main Principles

  • Patient information must remain confidential.
  • PHI may only be shared for permitted purposes or when authorized by the patient, unless another legal exception applies.
  • Patients have rights regarding their health information.
  • Healthcare organizations must train employees on privacy requirements.
Remember: Privacy Rule = Protects patient information from improper use or disclosure.

HIPAA Security Rule

The Security Rule focuses on protecting Electronic Protected Health Information (ePHI). It requires organizations to use administrative, physical, and technical safeguards.

Safeguard Purpose
Administrative Policies, employee training, and risk management.
Physical Protect buildings, computers, and devices.
Technical Passwords, encryption, firewalls, and secure access.

Protected Health Information (PHI)

Protected Health Information (PHI) is individually identifiable health information that relates to a person's health, healthcare services, or payment for healthcare.

Examples of PHI

  • Patient Name
  • Medical Record Number (MRN)
  • Date of Birth
  • Diagnosis
  • Treatment Details
  • Laboratory Reports
  • Insurance Information
  • Billing Records
  • Prescription Information

Electronic Protected Health Information (ePHI)

ePHI is PHI that is created, stored, transmitted, or received electronically.

Examples
Electronic Health Records (EHR)
Hospital Information Systems
Medical Billing Software
Electronic Claims
Electronic Remittance Advice (ERA)
Emails containing patient information

Covered Entities

Covered Entities are organizations that must comply with HIPAA requirements.

  • Healthcare Providers
  • Hospitals
  • Clinics
  • Health Insurance Companies
  • Healthcare Clearinghouses

Business Associates

A Business Associate is a person or organization that performs services for a Covered Entity and may have access to PHI while carrying out those services.

Examples

  • Medical Billing Companies
  • Medical Coding Companies
  • Cloud Storage Providers
  • IT Support Vendors
  • Claims Processing Companies
  • Data Backup Providers
Business Associates generally sign a Business Associate Agreement (BAA) with the Covered Entity, describing responsibilities for protecting PHI.

HIPAA Compliance Workflow

Patient Information ⬇ Collect PHI ⬇ Store Securely ⬇ Use Only for Authorized Purposes ⬇ Share Only When Permitted ⬇ Maintain Confidentiality ⬇ Protect Against Unauthorized Access
