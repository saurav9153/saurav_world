Health Insurance

Health insurance is a contract between the insurance company (payer) and the insured member (patient). It helps cover the cost of medical services such as doctor visits, surgeries, diagnostic tests, emergency care, and prescription medications.

Instead of paying the full medical bill, the patient shares the cost with the insurance company according to the policy benefits.

Explanation of Benefits (EOB)

An Explanation of Benefits (EOB) is a document sent by the insurance company to the patient after processing a claim.

It explains how the insurance company processed the claim, how much was paid, how much was adjusted, and how much the patient still owes.

EOB Includes

  • Patient Name
  • Provider Name
  • Date of Service (DOS)
  • Billed Amount
  • Allowed Amount
  • Insurance Payment
  • Patient Responsibility
  • Adjustment Reason Codes
  • Claim Status

Sample EOB

Description Amount ($)
Billed Amount 300
Allowed Amount 250
Insurance Paid 200
Patient Responsibility 50
Remember: EOB is NOT a bill. It only explains how the insurance company processed the claim.

Electronic Remittance Advice (ERA)

ERA stands for Electronic Remittance Advice. It is the electronic version of payment information sent by the insurance company directly to the healthcare provider.

ERA is mainly used by healthcare providers and medical billing professionals for posting insurance payments into billing software.

ERA Contains

  • Claim Number
  • Patient Information
  • Payment Amount
  • Adjustment Codes
  • Reason Codes
  • Denial Codes
  • Check / EFT Number
  • Payment Date

Patient Responsibility

Patient Responsibility is the amount the patient must pay after insurance processes the claim.

It may include:

  • Deductible
  • Copayment (Copay)
  • Coinsurance
  • Non-covered Services
  • Out-of-Network Charges

Example

Description Amount ($)
Total Hospital Bill 500
Insurance Paid 420
Patient Responsibility 80
Patient Responsibility does not always mean the insurance denied the claim. It can simply be the patient's deductible, copay, or coinsurance according to the insurance policy.

Deductible

A deductible is the amount a patient must pay each plan year before the insurance company starts paying for covered healthcare services.

Example

$500 $300 $0 $300
Remaining Deductible $200 $0 $200
Deductible Met $1,000 Policy Benefits Apply Only Copay/Coinsurance
Memory Tip: Deductible = Patient pays first.

Copayment (Copay)

A copayment is a fixed dollar amount the patient pays for a covered healthcare service.

Service Typical Copay
Primary Care Visit $25
Specialist Visit $50
Emergency Room $150
Prescription Drug $10 - $40
Memory Tip: Copay = Fixed amount.

Coinsurance

Coinsurance is the percentage of the allowed amount that the patient pays after meeting the deductible.

Insurance Patient
80% 20%

Example

Allowed Amount Insurance (80%) Patient (20%)
$1,000 $800 $200
Memory Tip: Coinsurance = Percentage after deductible.

Out-of-Pocket Maximum

The Out-of-Pocket Maximum is the highest amount a patient must pay during a policy year for covered services. After reaching this limit, the insurance plan generally pays 100% of covered services for the remainder of the plan year (subject to plan rules).

Annual Maximum Patient Already Paid Remaining
$6,000 $5,700 $300

Contractual Adjustment (Write-Off)

Insurance companies negotiate contracted rates with in-network providers. The difference between the provider's billed charge and the contracted allowed amount is called a contractual adjustment or write-off. Patients are generally not responsible for this amount when using in-network providers.

Billed Amount Allowed Amount Write-Off
$400 $300 $100

Coordination of Benefits (COB)

When a patient has more than one health insurance policy, Coordination of Benefits determines which insurance pays first (Primary Payer) and which pays second (Secondary Payer).

  • Primary Insurance pays first.
  • Secondary Insurance may pay some or all of the remaining balance according to its policy.

Prior Authorization

Prior Authorization (Pre-Authorization) is approval required from the insurance company before certain procedures, surgeries, medications, or diagnostic tests are performed.

Failure to obtain required authorization may result in claim denial.

Referral

A referral is a written or electronic recommendation from a Primary Care Physician (PCP) allowing a patient to receive care from a specialist. Some insurance plans require referrals before specialist visits.
